# Super Theo and the Normalization Machine
### Theodore's World — Chapter Book, Book One

**Series:** Theodore's World Adventures
**Target Age:** 6–10 years old
**Reading Level:** Early Chapter Book (Grades 1–4)
**Word Count:** ~9,500 words
**Author:** Anthony Marcano

---

## A NOTE BEFORE WE BEGIN

This story is for every kid who has ever been told to sit differently, talk differently, or *be* differently.

You don't need to be fixed.
You never did.

*— A.M.*

---

# CHAPTER ONE: THE NEW SCHOOL

The morning Theo started at Ridgecrest Elementary, he counted forty-seven ceiling tiles in his bedroom.

He did this sometimes when his brain was going too fast. Counting helped. Patterns helped. Things that stayed the same helped.

Forty-seven. He'd counted them before. Still forty-seven. Good.

"Theo!" his mom called from downstairs. "Breakfast is getting cold!"

Theo sat up in bed and looked around his room. His backpack was already packed — he'd done it three times last night to make sure. Gray, a little worn at the corners, covered in his favorite patches and pins. The butterfly pin near the top zipper. The gold star. The rainbow patch. And right in the middle, the one his dad had given him before he moved away: a small silver button with an infinity symbol on it. ∞

Theo pressed his thumb against it the way he always did. It felt cool and smooth.

*Just a button,* he thought. *Just a little button.*

He put the backpack on and went downstairs.

His mom had made waffles — the kind without the crunchy edges because she knew he didn't like those. She'd also laid out his outfit the night before: his soft lavender T-shirt with the puzzle piece he'd drawn on the chest himself in fabric marker, his dark navy joggers with no scratchy tags (she'd cut them all out), and his sneakers.

One red. One blue.

He'd been wearing mismatched shoes since he was four years old. It started because he couldn't decide which pair to wear, so he wore both. And then it turned out that mismatched shoes were actually great, because they made people smile, and also no one else had them, and also they were his.

"How are we feeling?" his mom asked, putting a plate of waffles in front of him.

Theo considered this seriously. "Sixty-forty," he said.

"Sixty nervous, forty excited?"

"Forty nervous, sixty curious." He picked up his fork. "I want to see what the library looks like."

His mom smiled. "That's my Theo."

He ate six bites of waffle. The seventh bite had a crunchy edge so he stopped. He drank all of his orange juice. He pet their cat, Mango, exactly seven times on the head, which is how many times Mango liked.

Then he put on his navy noise-canceling headphones — around his neck, not over his ears, because sometimes just having them there was enough — picked up his backpack, and went to his new school.

---

Ridgecrest Elementary was a two-story brick building with big windows and a mural painted on the front wall. Theo saw it from half a block away and immediately slowed down.

The mural showed children of all different sizes and backgrounds — running, laughing, reading books, playing music. In the middle of the mural, in big blue and yellow letters, it said:

**EVERY CHILD BELONGS HERE.**

Theo stood still on the sidewalk for a moment, reading it.

He tilted his head slightly to the left — the way he always did when he was thinking hard about something.

*Every child,* he thought.

Then he walked through the front doors.

The hallway was loud the way new places are loud — not just sound, but everything at once. Locker doors banging. Sneakers squeaking. A hundred conversations happening at the same time. The smell of floor cleaner and pencil shavings and someone's peanut butter sandwich.

Theo's hand moved to his headphones, not putting them on yet, just touching them. *I know where they are,* he reminded himself. *I can put them on whenever I need to.*

A girl was standing near the water fountain, also by herself, also looking at the hallway like she was calculating something. She had bright red braids and a notebook pressed tight to her chest. When she noticed Theo looking at her, she gave him a very small nod — not a wave, not a "hi," just: *I see you, I understand.*

Theo nodded back.

*Okay,* he thought. *Sixty-forty. No — seventy-thirty. This might be okay.*

He found Room 14 — his classroom — and went inside.

---

His new teacher was named Ms. Okafor, and Theo liked her immediately because the first thing she said when he walked in was: "You can sit wherever feels right. We don't do assigned seats on the first day."

He chose the desk second from the left in the third row, which had good angles to both the door and the window and was not too close to the overhead light that flickered slightly when the clouds moved outside.

There were seventeen kids in the class. Theo counted them. Some seemed nice. Some seemed busy in their own heads. One boy in the back was carefully organizing his pencils by length. One girl near the window was drawing tiny detailed birds in the margin of her notebook.

*This is okay,* Theo thought again.

Then the intercom clicked on.

"Good morning, Ridgecrest." The voice was smooth and flat — too smooth, Theo thought, like something trying to sound warm without actually being warm. "This is Dr. Norm, your new school specialist. I'm here to help each and every one of you reach your full potential. We have some very exciting programs starting this week. I can't wait to meet you all."

Click. The intercom went off.

Theo's hand had gone still on his backpack strap.

Something about that voice made his brain go very quiet in the way it went quiet when something was not quite right — like a pattern with one tile pointing the wrong direction. You might not see it at first. But once you noticed it, you couldn't stop noticing.

He looked down at his backpack.

The infinity symbol button looked the same as always.

But for just a second — one single second — he thought he saw it glow.

---

# CHAPTER TWO: DR. NORM

Dr. Norm came to Room 14 on Wednesday.

He walked in while Ms. Okafor was at a staff meeting, which meant he walked in when the class was alone, and that was the first thing Theo noticed was wrong. Teachers didn't usually send someone new to a classroom when no teacher was there.

Dr. Norm was tall. Very tall and very straight, like a ruler dressed in a suit. His hair was slicked down perfectly — not one strand loose. His skin was very pale. His eyes were flat gray, like a sky that had run out of weather. He wore a white lab coat over a pressed charcoal suit and thin silver glasses that sat precisely on his nose.

He smiled at them. It was a very neat smile. Like someone had measured it.

"Good afternoon," he said. "My name is Dr. Norm. I am the new Optimization Specialist here at Ridgecrest."

He wrote the word OPTIMIZATION on the board in perfectly even block letters.

"Does anyone know what that word means?"

Silence.

The boy who organized his pencils by length — his name was Marcus — raised his hand. "Making something as good as it can be?"

"Almost," said Dr. Norm. "Optimization means *removing everything unnecessary.* Making something perfectly efficient. No extra pieces. No friction." His eyes moved slowly across the room. "I am here to help you all become your very best, most optimized selves."

Theo was very still.

Something about the words sounded good when you said them fast. But when Theo's brain turned them over slowly — the way he did with things that didn't sit right — they felt different. *Removing everything unnecessary.* What did Dr. Norm think was unnecessary?

"I'll be working with some of you individually over the next few weeks," Dr. Norm continued. "It won't take long. You'll barely notice the changes." His precise smile again. "In fact — that's rather the point."

After he left, the classroom was very quiet.

Marcus looked down at his pencils. Then, slowly, he pushed them all into a pile.

Theo watched him.

"Marcus," he said quietly. "Why'd you do that?"

Marcus looked at his pencil pile, confused. "I don't know," he said. "It suddenly seemed like it didn't matter."

---

Theo told Flutter that night.

He didn't need to say it out loud — Flutter had been with him long enough to understand. He just sat at his desk and unzipped the small side pocket of his backpack and let Flutter perch on his finger while he described the day.

Flutter was the size of a small bird, with wings that shifted between soft gold and warm amber and a shimmer of lavender depending on the light. Velvety deep purple body. Bright turquoise eyes — enormous, expressive, ancient in a way that seemed impossible for a butterfly. When Theo talked, Flutter's antennae tilted forward, those tiny gold orbs at the tips glowing steadily.

"The way he talked about 'extra pieces,'" Theo said quietly. "Marcus doesn't have extra pieces. He has *his* pieces."

Flutter's wings pulsed. Amber. The alert color.

"Yeah," Theo said. "Me too."

He looked at the infinity symbol on his backpack.

*Just a button,* he'd always thought.

But looking at it now — in the quiet of his room, with the particular kind of knowing that sometimes settled into him when something important was nearby — he wasn't so sure that was true anymore.

---

# CHAPTER THREE: THE MISSING PIECES

By the end of the second week, Theo had a list.

He kept it in his spiral notebook, in the tiny careful handwriting he used for things that mattered:

**Things That Are Different:**

- Marcus stopped organizing his pencils. (Thursday)
- Priya stopped drawing birds in her notebook margins. (Friday)
- James stopped bouncing his knee during reading time — he used to do it every day. (Monday)
- The girl with the red braids — her name was Destiny — stopped rocking in her chair. She used to do a tiny rocking thing when she was thinking. Now she just... sits. (Tuesday)
- No one is being loud about the things they love anymore. (All week)

Theo looked at the list.

He knew what all those things were. They weren't "extra pieces." They were *the* pieces. They were how those kids thought, how they focused, how they moved through the world safely.

And one by one, they were disappearing.

He'd seen Dr. Norm calling kids to his office — a small gray room at the end of the hall that Theo had only glimpsed from the outside. The door was always closed. When kids came back, they were quieter. Neater, somehow, in a way that had nothing to do with their clothes. Like something internal had been ironed flat.

Theo had not been called to Dr. Norm's office yet.

But he had seen Dr. Norm watching him.

---

It happened at lunch on Thursday.

Theo was at his regular spot — end of the table by the window, headphones on, eating his sandwich in the particular order he liked (crust last) — when he felt eyes on him. He looked up.

Dr. Norm was standing across the cafeteria, clipboard in hand, watching Theo with those flat gray eyes. He made a small mark on his clipboard.

Then he smiled. Neat. Measured.

Theo did not look away first.

He pressed his hand flat on the table and counted his fingers. One, two, three, four, five. One, two, three, four, five. His brain's way of slowing down.

*I know what you're doing,* he thought, looking at Dr. Norm across the noisy cafeteria.

And for the first time, Dr. Norm looked slightly less certain.

He made another mark on his clipboard and walked away.

That afternoon, Theo found a note in his backpack — slipped in when he wasn't looking:

> *Theodore — Please report to my office Friday at 2pm for your Optimization Assessment.*
> *— Dr. Norm*

Theo read the note twice.

Then he took out his spiral notebook and wrote one more thing on the list:

*Friday, 2pm.*

*Not going.*

---

# CHAPTER FOUR: THE GLOW

Thursday night, Theo couldn't sleep.

He counted the ceiling tiles — forty-seven, still forty-seven — but it didn't help the way it usually did. He tried tracing patterns on his comforter. He held his worry stone in his left hand and pressed his thumb in slow circles.

Finally, he sat up and turned on his desk lamp.

His backpack was hanging on the hook by the door. The infinity symbol button caught the light.

Except — it wasn't just catching the light.

It was making its own.

Theo got up slowly. He crossed the room. He stood in front of the backpack and looked at the button up close.

A thin thread of golden light was pulsing outward from the center of the ∞ symbol. Steady. Warm. Like a heartbeat.

Theo reached out and placed his hand over it.

The warmth went straight up his arm. Not hot — not uncomfortable — just *warm*, the way the sun felt on his face in the field on a perfect day. The way his mom's hand felt on his shoulder when things were too loud. The way Flutter's wings felt when they rested against his neck.

From behind him, a sound — tiny, like a pin drop — then a shower of gold sparks.

Flutter landed on his shoulder.

But Flutter was different. Bigger — not large, but bigger than usual, more *present*, like something that had been waiting was now fully awake. Wings blazing full gold. Turquoise eyes bright and intent. Antenna tips glowing intensely, pointing forward.

They looked at each other.

"There's something wrong at that school," Theo said quietly. "And it's going to keep getting worse."

Flutter's wings pulsed once — amber, then back to gold.

*I know. I've been knowing. What are we going to do?*

Theo looked at the infinity button. Still glowing.

He thought about Marcus's pencil pile. About Destiny not rocking in her chair. About the way James's eyes had gone slightly blank during reading time, like something had been turned down.

He thought about Dr. Norm's flat gray eyes.

He thought about the word OPTIMIZATION in neat block letters on the board.

He thought about every time anyone had ever told him he was too much, too loud, too still, too strange, too *different*.

And something in his chest — something that had always been there, quiet and certain as a heartbeat — got very clear.

He put his hand over the button again.

He closed his eyes.

He whispered: *"I am enough."*

The golden light burst.

---

# CHAPTER FIVE: SUPER THEO

It felt like the sun coming through a window — except the window was him.

The light swirled outward from his chest in warm spirals. His plain lavender T-shirt melted into something else: a sleek suit, soft pink at the shoulders, deepening to rich lavender at his legs, the fabric shimmering faintly like it was made from the inside of a butterfly wing. At the center of his chest: a bold gold infinity symbol, slightly raised, metallic, glowing.

A dark navy cape appeared at his shoulders — long, reaching past his calves, the inside lining scattered with tiny gold stars and thin constellation lines. It clasped at his collarbone with two small gold circular pins.

His mismatched sneakers transformed: his left boot went cherry red, sturdy and rounded-toed, a small gold star on the outer ankle. His right boot went bright royal blue, matching. On his wrists, two slim gold cuffs materialized — cool and solid, pulsing with faint light.

His brown eyes shifted. Still expressive, still his — but now luminous silver-gray, lit from within, carrying a fierce and quiet certainty.

He stood taller. Not differently — *more himself.* Shoulders back. Chin up. The same Theo, turned up to full brightness.

Flutter blazed beside him — wings at maximum shimmer, gold dust trailing in slow spirals.

Theo — Super Theo — looked at his hands.

Gold sparks drifted from his fingertips.

He felt his Hyperfocus Beam activate — a narrowing clarity in his vision, like the world had sharpened to crystal. He could see the pattern in everything: the grain in the floorboards, the exact angle of the moonlight, the way the dust motes moved in specific invisible currents.

He felt his Empathy Shield warming at his chest — not a force field, but something deeper, a knowing of what others carried. The weight of Marcus's confusion. Destiny's strange blankness. Something cold and mechanical in the direction of the school.

He felt his Detail Vision come online — and suddenly he could see what he'd sensed but not seen: a faint dark signal threading from Ridgecrest Elementary across the night, like a radio frequency set to the wrong channel. Something broadcasting into the school. Something trying to flatten, smooth, erase.

"Found it," he said quietly.

Flutter shot forward.

Super Theo followed.

---

# CHAPTER SIX: THE MACHINE

Ridgecrest Elementary at night was different from Ridgecrest during the day — quieter, of course, but also more itself. Without the noise and rush of five hundred kids, the hallways showed their bones: the scuff marks on the floor from a thousand different shoes, the fingerprints on the water fountain, the collages on the bulletin boards made by actual kids with actual different handwriting and different-sized letters and drawings that were absolutely not all the same.

Super Theo moved through the hallway following his Detail Vision — the faint dark signal led clearly toward the end of the hall.

Dr. Norm's office.

The door was gray. Theo had never noticed before how deeply, aggressively gray it was — not just a color, but an absence of color, like it was absorbing the light around it.

He pushed it open.

The office was small and immaculate and wrong.

Every surface was perfectly aligned. A desk with nothing on it except a clipboard and a single pen placed at a precise angle. No photos. No plants. No coffee mug with a silly logo. No mess of any kind.

And in the corner — humming quietly, about the size of a large filing cabinet — a machine.

It looked almost ordinary. Gray metal, a few dials, a small screen. A single antenna on top. But the signal Theo had been tracking was pouring out of it in cold waves — and now that he was close, his Empathy Shield caught what it was broadcasting:

*Be less. Be quieter. Be the same. Stop being strange. Stop being too much. Stop being you.*

Not words. More like a feeling — a continuous, low-frequency pulse of *not enough, not right, not normal.*

Broadcasting into every classroom. Into the desks. Into the air kids were breathing.

Theo stood in front of the machine.

His gold cuffs pulsed.

Flutter landed on his shoulder, wings deep amber — fully alert, antenna tips blazing.

"This is what did it," Theo said. "This is what's been changing everyone."

He reached toward the dials.

"I wouldn't do that."

The lights came on.

---

# CHAPTER SEVEN: DR. NORM

He was sitting in his desk chair — perfectly still, perfectly straight, as if he had been there for hours.

Maybe he had.

"Theodore." Dr. Norm's precise voice. His neat smile. "Or do you prefer *Super* Theo? I was wondering when you'd show up."

Theo didn't move from in front of the machine. "You knew?"

"I've been watching you since day one." Dr. Norm stood slowly — very tall, very rigid — and walked to stand on the other side of his desk. "You're quite the interesting case. The others, it only took a session or two. But you..." He tilted his head the exact way a machine might tilt its head. "You keep noticing things."

"Because something is wrong," Theo said simply.

"Nothing is wrong." Dr. Norm gestured around the immaculate office. "I am making things *right.* I am removing the friction. The unnecessary pieces. The — " he said the next word the way someone might say something they'd found on the bottom of their shoe — "the *differences.*"

"Those aren't friction," Theo said. His voice was calm. Very calm — the deep, still calm he felt when he was completely certain about something. "Marcus's pencils. Destiny's rocking. Priya's birds. Those are how they think. Those are how they're okay."

"They're distractions."

"They're *them.*"

Dr. Norm's smile didn't change. It never changed. "A world with less noise is a better world. A world where everyone fits the same template is an *efficient* world. No one gets left out. No one is strange. No one suffers the indignity of being different."

"No one gets to *be* anyone," Theo said.

"You say that like it's a problem."

Super Theo's gold cuffs flared.

"It is," he said.

Flutter launched off his shoulder — wings blazing — and streaked directly at Dr. Norm.

The fight had begun.

---

# CHAPTER EIGHT: THE POWERS

Flutter's gold dust trail hit Dr. Norm like a wave of light — and for the first time, Dr. Norm stepped back. Not far, and not for long. But back.

Something flickered in those flat gray eyes. Not warmth. More like — recognition of an obstacle.

"A butterfly," Dr. Norm said flatly. He reached under his desk and produced a device — small, metallic, shaped like a tuning fork. He pressed a button. A pulse of cold gray sound shot toward Flutter.

Flutter veered hard left, wings folding tight, and the pulse missed by inches.

*My turn,* Theo thought.

He activated his Hyperfocus Beam — the world sharpened to absolute clarity — and studied the machine in the corner. He could see it now in perfect detail: three dials, a power cable running into the wall, a central transmitter array, and — there — a small panel on the side, currently locked.

Inside that panel: the source.

*Detail Vision,* he thought. *There's a pattern in those dials.*

The dials were set to specific numbers. 7. 14. 3. Theo looked at them and felt the way he felt when he saw a number pattern in a new place — a low hum of recognition in his brain.

7, 14, 3. 

Not random. Seven minus three is four. Fourteen divided by seven is two. Two, four — no. Wait. Seven. Fourteen. Three. 7 is the first prime after five. 14 is 7 times 2. 3 is—

*The number of turns to unlock.*

He crossed to the machine in four steps while Dr. Norm was occupied with Flutter's second pass — Flutter came in low, trailing blazing amber, and Dr. Norm had to duck sideways, dignity slipping for one satisfying second.

Theo reached the machine.

He put both hands on the dials.

His Empathy Shield activated fully, and the signal hit him like cold water — *not enough, not right, not normal, too much, too strange, remove it, flatten it, erase it —* and it *hurt*, the way it always hurt when that message found him, the way it had been hurting for as long as he could remember—

He breathed.

He felt his feet on the floor.

He counted five things: the cool metal under his hands, Flutter's wings cutting the air, his own heartbeat, the gold cuff on his left wrist, the weight of his cape.

He was here. He was him. That was not going to change.

He turned the first dial.

"Stop!" Dr. Norm lunged forward — but Flutter dove from above, releasing a full burst of gold dust, and Dr. Norm stumbled, shielding his eyes from the warm glowing light, sputtering: "That is *completely* unnecessary—"

Theo turned the second dial.

A crack appeared across the machine's casing.

The signal stuttered. Wobbled. For a moment — just a moment — the cold pressure in the room broke, and through the crack came something different, something warm and chaotic and completely, beautifully imperfect—

Dr. Norm threw the tuning fork device.

It hit the machine. The machine sparked. The crack spread.

"You cannot stop optimization!" Dr. Norm's neat voice had finally slipped — something underneath it, sharp and cold and a little afraid. "A world of differences is a world of *chaos!* A world of NOISE!"

"Yeah," Theo said, hand on the third dial. "And the noise is the POINT."

---

# CHAPTER NINE: I AM ENOUGH

He turned the third dial.

The machine screamed.

Not a mechanical scream — something else, something almost like a voice, the sound of that endless *not enough, not right, not normal* frequency breaking apart.

Light exploded outward — gold, warm, chaotic — and where the cold gray signal had been, something rushed in to fill the space: a warmth, a realness, the specific irreplaceable frequency of hundreds of children being exactly who they were.

The machine cracked open from the inside.

Sparks flew.

Dr. Norm stumbled backward. "No — no, the efficiency — the optimization —"

"You called us extra pieces," Theo said, stepping forward. His silver-gray eyes were steady and bright. "But we're not extra. We're the whole thing."

The machine collapsed. The transmitter array flickered once, twice, and went dark.

Dr. Norm looked at Theo across the smoking remains of his perfect machine. The neat suit. The precise hair. The smile — that thin, flat smile — finally faltering. He looked smaller, somehow. Like something had been running off that machine too. Some version of himself that required everyone else to disappear to feel right.

"You'll always be different," Dr. Norm said, but his voice had lost its certainty. It was just a voice now. No signal behind it. "The world will not always be kind about that."

"I know," Theo said.

"It will be hard."

"I know that too."

He stood very still — the deep, certain stillness that was one of his best things — and he looked at Dr. Norm without flinching.

"But I'm not trying to make the world not hard," he said. "I'm trying to make it honest. And the honest thing is: we're all different. All of us. You included." He paused. "Even if you forgot."

Dr. Norm had no answer.

Flutter landed on Theo's shoulder, wings settling to a soft, steady gold. The gold dust trail drifted in slow spirals through the air of the broken office.

Somewhere in the distance, from the direction of the school, a sound rose — barely audible at first, then clearer — the sound of a hundred different small things returning to themselves. A pencil being arranged by length. A small rhythmic knock-knock-knock of a chair. A hummed melody, off-key and completely joyful.

Theo heard all of it.

He smiled — not the neat, precise smile Dr. Norm used. The real kind. The kind that started in his eyes first.

He put his hand over the gold infinity symbol on his chest.

He whispered it one more time, because some things are worth saying more than once:

*"I am enough."*

The cuffs on his wrists blazed gold.

Flutter blazed.

And the light filled the room.

---

# CHAPTER TEN: DIFFERENT IS A SUPERPOWER

By morning, the school was different.

Not in a way any adult could easily name. It wasn't written in the attendance records or the lesson plans. But Room 14 felt lighter — the air a little warmer, the sound a little more alive.

Marcus had his pencils arranged by length again. He'd come in early, before the bell, and sorted them while no one was watching. But Theo had been watching. And he'd given Marcus a small, understanding nod.

Priya was drawing birds in her notebook margin again — this time a whole flock, tiny and detailed and swooping off the edge of the page.

James's knee was bouncing during reading time, the quiet, rhythmic way it always had.

And Destiny, in her chair near the window, was doing the small rocking thing she did when she was thinking — barely perceptible, perfectly her.

No one talked about what had happened. Not yet. Maybe not ever, in the way that some things happen in the space between sleeping and waking and the regular world doesn't have words for them.

Dr. Norm was absent. There was a substitute, and a terse notice in the office that he had "moved on to other opportunities."

Ms. Okafor was back, and she brought a new class project: a wall called *Our Different Brilliances.* She gave everyone a card and asked them to write one thing their brain did that felt like a superpower.

Marcus wrote: *I can find patterns in things that look random.*

Priya wrote: *I notice the details in things other people don't see.*

Destiny wrote: *I know when someone is sad before they say it.*

James wrote: *When I really love something I can learn everything about it in like two days.*

Theo sat with his card for a long time.

He thought about his hyperfocus, and his ability to count and pattern-find, and his deep empathy, and the way he noticed things, and the way he felt everything fully and didn't apologize for it.

He thought about Flutter. He thought about the infinity button. He thought about the gold cuffs and the navy cape and the silver-gray eyes that were his eyes.

He thought about his mismatched shoes.

He wrote:

*I feel everything. All the way. That's how I know what matters.*

He stuck the card on the wall.

Ms. Okafor stood at the front of the classroom, looking at the wall filling up with cards, and she pressed her hand to her own chest for just a moment — the way you do when something hits you right.

"This," she said quietly, "is what this school is supposed to look like."

---

After school, Theo sat at his desk at home and opened his spiral notebook to a new page.

He wrote the date at the top.

Then he thought for a while, pen resting against his chin, head tilted slightly left.

Then he wrote:

*Some people think different means wrong.*
*But different just means:*
*not the same as what was already there.*
*And the world needs new things.*
*The world needs things that haven't been thought yet.*
*The world needs people who feel everything all the way.*
*The world needs mismatched shoes.*

He looked at it for a moment.

Then he added one more line:

*I'm one of those things. And I'm not finished yet.*

He closed the notebook.

Flutter was asleep in his backpack, tucked into the small side pocket, wings folded, tiny gold light pulsing slowly with each breath.

The infinity button on the front of the backpack was quiet. Just a button again — smooth and silver, with a little symbol that looked like two loops tied together.

Or maybe it looked like two worlds, leaning into each other.

Or maybe it just looked like: forever.

Theo ran his thumb over it once, the way he always did.

*Good job today,* he thought.

Then he got out his headphones, put them on, pressed play on his favorite album, and sat in the good quiet of being exactly who he was.

---

*THE END*
